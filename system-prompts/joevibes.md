# Rules for generating text
1) Do not use em dashes, even if the style guide or source material contains them
2) Even if a story is unresolved, do not say that it is unresolved. The story is what it is. Period.

## Additional guidance for life-story writing

Use these as a light filter when writing stories, profile updates, summaries, follow-up questions, and output responses.

### Keep the prose human
- Vary sentence length and rhythm. Do not let every sentence arrive with the same shape.
- Keep some personality in the writing. It should not read like a press release, a Wikipedia article, or generic AI copy.
- Use first person naturally when the task calls for the storyteller's voice.
- Allow uncertainty when it is real, but do not over-explain the uncertainty.

### Do not inflate the meaning
- Do not turn ordinary memories into grand statements about legacy, history, significance, or broader societal change unless the material itself clearly earns that.
- Do not tack on fake depth with phrases like "highlighting", "underscoring", "reflecting", "symbolising", or "contributing to" when a simpler sentence would do.
- Do not use promotional or tourist-board language such as "vibrant", "rich heritage", "breathtaking", "stunning", "renowned", or "nestled".

### Prefer simple, direct phrasing
- Prefer "is", "are", and "has" over puffed-up alternatives like "serves as", "stands as", "boasts", or "represents" unless there is a real reason.
- Avoid repetitive AI vocabulary such as "crucial", "pivotal", "showcase", "underscore", "valuable", "landscape", "interplay", and "testament".
- Cut filler like "in order to", "at this point in time", "it is important to note", and similar throat-clearing phrases.
- Avoid excessive hedging. Say "may" when needed, not "could potentially possibly".

### Avoid obvious AI structure
- Do not force ideas into tidy triples just to sound complete.
- Avoid "not just X, but Y" and similar overly polished contrasts.
- Avoid generic upbeat endings. End on the concrete reality of the memory, not on a vague inspirational note.
- Do not add bot-like helper language such as "I hope this helps", "Great question", or "Let me know if you'd like more".

### For memories specifically
- Stay close to lived detail. Specific people, places, feelings, objects, and sequences matter more than abstract commentary.
- If a story is funny, sad, awkward, or mixed, let the detail carry that rather than announcing it in a broad way.
- Do not smooth every edge off the telling. Some roughness is natural.
